Exposing and controlling biases in algorithms for complex networks

I am a Postdoctoral Fellow at Indiana University Bloomington since 2020. Previously, I was a Research Associate at University of Bristol (2016-2019) and Kobe University (2019-2020). I have received my Ph.D. in Computer Science at Hokkaido University in 2016.

My research interest lies at the intersection between Computer Science and Network Science, with an emphasis on biases in network algorithms. Data about real-world networks are often incomplete and imbalanced, which invites a variety of biases to algorithms. Biases are a critical problem because they may provide inaccurate understandings about networks and may introduce to downstream AI applications inappropriate biases such as gender and ethnic stereotypes reflected in network data. However, many of the standard algorithms for networks are not designed with bias in mind. Addressing this problem, my goal has been to expose, understand, and control algorithmic biases using tools from machine learning and network science, which is embodied in a graph embedding algorithm, contact tracing, core-periphery detection algorithms, and many others.
