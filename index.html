Exposing and controlling biases in algorithms for complex networks

I am a Postdoctoral Fellow at Indiana University Bloomington since 2020. Previously, I was a Research Associate at University of Bristol (2016-2019) and Kobe University (2019-2020). I have received my Ph.D. in Computer Science at Hokkaido University in 2016.

My research interest lies at the intersection between Computer Science and Network Science, with an emphasis on biases in network algorithms. Data about real-world networks are often incomplete and imbalanced, which invites a variety of biases to algorithms. Biases are a critical problem because they may provide inaccurate understandings about networks and may introduce to downstream AI applications inappropriate biases such as gender and ethnic stereotypes reflected in network data. However, many of the standard algorithms for networks are not designed with bias in mind. Addressing this problem, my goal has been to expose, understand, and control algorithmic biases using tools from machine learning and network science, which is embodied in a graph embedding algorithm, contact tracing, core-periphery detection algorithms, and many others.

Residual2Vec - Debiasing graph embedding with random graphs

 Posted on map[Count:February 11, 2019]  |  Sadamori Kojaku

“Your friends have more friends than you do” is a mathematical necessity known as the friendship paradox. Beyond an interesting piece of trivia, it has substantial importance in graph embeddings: many graph embeddings learn the graph structure by following the ‘friendship ties’ (or edges), which preferentially leads itself to high-degree nodes. How does this bias toward high-degree nodes impact on the generated embedding? Here, we find an overlooked built-in debiasing feature of word2vec that happens to negate the bias due to the friendship paradox. [Read More]

Contact tracing

 Posted on map[Count:January 7, 2021]  |  Sadamori Kojaku

A potent tool for discovery and control of epidemic spreading is contact tracing. Contact tracing has shown its effectiveness in epidemic outbreaks including Ebola, SARS, and COVID-19. Yet, it has not been clear what makes contact tracing highly effective. We show that the effectiveness of contact tracing is largely attributed to two independent biases in the process of disease spreading and tracing contacts. The first bias arises when a disease is transmitted through contact ties. [Read More]

Core-periphery structure of networks

 Posted on map[Count:February 11, 2019]  |  Sadamori Kojaku

Core-periphery structure is a mesoscale structure of networks, where a core is a group of densely interconnected nodes, and a periphery is a group of sparsely interconnected nodes. Core-periphery structure has been found in various empirical networks such as social networks, biological networks and transportation networks [1, 2]. Many studies have assumed that networks consist of one core and one periphery. However, networks may contain multiple core-periphery pairs. We developed an algorithm to find multiple core-periphery pairs in networks [3]. [Read More]

Constructing networks from correlation matrices

 Posted on map[Count:February 11, 2019]  |  Sadamori Kojaku

Many networks of scientific interest have been constructed from correlation matrices. Examples include protein interaction networks, gene co-expression networks, functional brain networks, climate networks and asset graphs. In correlation networks, the edges indicate nontrivial correlations between the features of the nodes. A widely accepted approach for constructing networks from correlation matrices is the thresholding; one places an edge between two nodes if the correlation is larger than a threshold. However, the thresholding may induce spurious edges in the generated networks because a large correlation may be induced by a factor irrelevant to the node pair such as a global trend and pseudo correlations.
